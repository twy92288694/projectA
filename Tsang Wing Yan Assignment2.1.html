Culture and Heritage

Culture

The Philippines boasts a vibrant culture shaped by indigenous traditions, Spanish colonization, and modern influences. Indigenous groups like the Badjao, known as "sea gypsies," have a deep connection to the ocean, relying on free-diving and spearfishing for centuries. Spanish rule (1565–1898) introduced Catholicism, seen in colorful fiestas like Sinulog and Ati-Atihan.

Filipino hospitality is legendary—divers will feel welcomed with warm smiles and a strong sense of community (bayanihan). The cuisine blends Malay, Spanish, and Chinese flavors, with seafood dishes like kinilaw (ceviche) and sinigang (sour soup) being local favorites.

Music and dance are integral to Filipino culture, with traditional performances like the tinikling (bamboo dance) often showcased at beach resorts. English is widely spoken, making communication easy for visitors.

For divers, understanding local customs—such as respecting marine sanctuaries and supporting sustainable fishing practices—enhances the experience while preserving the Philippines' rich cultural and natural heritage.

History

The Philippines' history is a tapestry of indigenous kingdoms, colonial rule, and hard-won independence. Before Spanish arrival in 1521, maritime trade flourished with neighboring Asian empires. Spanish colonization (1565–1898) brought Christianity, fortresses like Intramuros, and the galleon trade—linking Manila to Acapulco.

After the Spanish-American War (1898), the U.S. took control, introducing English and modern infrastructure. World War II left scars, with battles like Leyte Gulf shaping the nation’s path to independence in 1946.

The Philippines’ diving spots hold historical significance—wrecks like WWII ships in Coron and Subic Bay offer underwater time capsules. Indigenous maritime traditions, such as the Badjao’s free-diving heritage, highlight the deep ties between Filipinos and the sea.

Today, the Philippines balances preservation and progress, with marine sanctuaries protecting biodiversity while welcoming divers to explore its rich history—both above and below the waves.

Weather

Dry Season (November–May)
– Best for Diving

  • ☀️ Sunny skies, calm seas, excellent visibility (often 20–40 meters)
  • 📅 Peak diving months: December–April (cooler, less humidity)
  • 🌡️ March–May: Warmer water (28–30°C/82–86°F), ideal for macro diving

Wet Season (June–October)
– Variable Conditions

  • 🌧️ Monsoon rains & occasional storms, but some regions remain diveable
  • 🌊 West (Palawan, Luzon): Rough seas (Southwest monsoon)
  • ⛱️ East (Davao, Siargao): Calmer (protected from winds)
  • 👀 Visibility: Can drop to 10–20m after heavy rain
